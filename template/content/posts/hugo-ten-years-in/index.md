---
title: "Hugo, ten years in"
date: 2026-02-27
---

I have been using Hugo for long enough that the version numbers have lost their meaning to me. Releases come and go and most of the time the only thing that changes is that something I did not realise was awkward suddenly is not awkward anymore.

The thing that has aged best is the build speed. The thing I did not appreciate at the start is how much that speed compounds. Fast builds make you write differently. You experiment more. You delete more. You do not commit to a structure prematurely because trying a new one costs you nothing.

The other thing that has aged well is the willingness to break things when breaking them is the right move. It is rare and good.

What has aged less well, on my side, is the way I used to build sites. The first Hugo site I made in 2016 had a templating structure I would not write today. It was clever in the way that early-career cleverness is clever: it solved a problem I did not have at the cost of making the actual problems harder to solve. Every couple of years I would refactor it, and every refactor would remove a layer of cleverness and replace it with something boring. The site is now, by any reasonable measure, dull. It also takes me thirty seconds to make a change, instead of an afternoon, and that has been the best trade I have ever made on a personal codebase.

The other thing I keep noticing is how much of the project's character comes from a small number of opinionated decisions made early. Content as files, not a database. Markdown as the source of truth. Templates as plain text. None of these are revolutionary in 2026, but the willingness to commit to them and then live inside the consequences for ten years is the thing that I now think of as the project's actual achievement. Most software does not get that. Most software gets pulled in fifteen directions by the first thousand users and ends up shaped like a committee.

Hugo is not shaped like a committee. That is most of what I would tell someone considering it now.
