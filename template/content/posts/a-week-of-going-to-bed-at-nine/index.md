---
title: "What I learned from a week of going to bed at nine"
date: 2026-01-14
---

A friend I respect mentioned, almost in passing, that he had started going to bed at nine in the evening and getting up at five. He said it was the best decision he had made in a decade. I am suspicious of decade-best decisions, but I tried it for a week.

The friend in question has, by his own account, made some excellent decisions in the past decade and some genuinely terrible ones. I asked him which others belonged on the same shelf, and he listed three, of which two had to do with what time he went to bed and one had to do with selling a car. So I took the recommendation seriously, but with calibration.

Day one, I lay in bed from nine to about twenty past eleven, alternately checking my phone and listing reasons I should not have agreed to this. Day two was worse, because I had now accumulated a small sleep debt, and the body, given a chance to fall asleep at nine, used the chance to revisit its grievances. Day three I woke up, briefly, at some implausible hour and was so confused about what time it was that I checked my phone three times before accepting it. The phone said three forty-seven. I was furious, in a quiet way, with my friend, my body, and my decision-making.

Day four was the shift. I do not know what changed mechanically, but at four-thirty I woke up clear-headed and not particularly tired, and instead of arguing with myself about it I made coffee and sat at the kitchen table with a book. The kitchen at four-thirty is a different kitchen. It does not have a refrigerator hum. It does not have anyone else's plans in it. It is, for two hours, a kitchen that belongs to whoever is awake, and on Thursday and Friday and Saturday that was me.

By Sunday I had read the first hundred and twenty pages of a book I had been carrying around since November. I had also written a long email I had been avoiding for a week, paid two bills that needed paying, and felt, for the first time in a long time, that I had begun the day on my own terms before anyone could ask anything of me.

Then on Monday I had a meeting at eight in the evening that ran late and the whole structure collapsed.

What stuck is something less ambitious. I leave the laptop downstairs now, in a basket by the door, and I do not look at it after dinner. The five-in-the-morning wake-up is gone. The quiet morning is gone. But the small habit underneath it, the part where I put a fence around the part of the day that belongs to me, that part stayed.
