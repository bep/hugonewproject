---
title: "A small ritual: writing a note before opening the editor"
date: 2026-03-19
---

For the past month I have been writing one sentence in a paper notebook before I open my editor in the morning. Just one sentence about what I plan to do that day. It takes about thirty seconds.

I did not plan for this to become a ritual. It started as a way to stop myself from getting pulled into email before I had a single line of intention for the day. But the side effect is that I now have a small written record of every working day in March, and reading it back is more useful than I expected.

The notes are unromantic. *Finish the migration. Reply to J. Read the spec.* What surprises me is how often I would have forgotten the day entirely without them.

The first entry I wrote, on a Monday in February, said only *Find out why the build is slow.* I did not find out why the build was slow. I have, however, written that same sentence three more times since, and reading them in sequence is unsettling. I did not realize, at the time, that I was failing to fix the same thing for two months in a row. The notebook noticed. I did not.

The other thing the notebook does is calibrate. When I write *Finish the spec by lunch* and discover at three in the afternoon that I have not started, I have a piece of evidence about my own estimation that no calendar will give me. The notebook does not lecture. It just sits there, with the morning's plan on it, while the day plays out underneath.

I have started using a different page for weekends, although I have nothing planned on weekends. The page becomes a record of what I did instead, written in past tense. *Walked to the river. Read for an hour. Spent too long talking to the neighbour about hedges.* I do not know yet what these are for. I suspect that, like the weekday entries, they will become useful only in retrospect, and only by accident.
