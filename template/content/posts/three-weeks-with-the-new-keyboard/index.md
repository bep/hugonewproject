---
title: "Three weeks with the new keyboard, and one quiet observation about typing rhythm I did not expect"
date: 2026-04-21
---

The keyboard arrived on a Monday. I had read enough reviews to know what to expect from the switches and the layout, but nothing prepares you for the first hour of typing on something genuinely different. The first day was full of small mistakes. By the third I had stopped noticing them.

What I did not expect is the change in pacing. With the old board I would type in long bursts, then pause, then type in long bursts again. The new one has me writing in shorter, more even runs. I do not think it is the switches. I think it is the slightly higher activation force, which makes the act of pressing a key feel more like a decision.

It is the kind of thing I would have dismissed as a placebo if someone else had reported it. Three weeks in, I am no longer sure that matters.

I bought it after spending a long evening reading other people write about keyboards. There is a certain kind of writing on the internet, mostly forum threads, where people describe the feel of objects in language that is closer to wine reviews than to product reviews. I have always found this slightly silly. I now suspect the silliness was mine, and not theirs. The vocabulary is what is silly. The thing being described is real.

The first week I made a list of every small problem I noticed. The home row was different. The function keys were nowhere near where my fingers expected them. The escape key required a deliberate reach. I would type a sentence and notice halfway through that I had been holding my hands at a slightly wrong angle for the entire morning. By Friday, the list had stopped growing. By the second Monday, I could not remember what was on it.

The thing I keep coming back to is the activation force. It is not heavy, but it is heavier than what I am used to. The old board was so light that I would sometimes register a key press while reading, by accident, just from the weight of my hand resting on the surface. That does not happen now. To type a letter I have to mean it. There is a small floor of intention required, and the result is that I think about what I am writing one half-thought sooner than I used to.

It is not faster. I am not faster. I checked, because I expected to be slower and wanted to know how much. I am within a few percent of where I was. The output, though, is different. There are fewer half-finished sentences in my drafts. Fewer reaches for words I did not need. I do not know if this is because the keyboard is forcing me to commit, or because I am paying more attention because the keyboard is new, or because I have been writing about the keyboard for three weeks and writing about something tends to make you write better. All three are plausible. I do not know how to separate them.

The keyboard is loud. Not unpleasantly, but more than I expected. The first day my partner asked, with some patience, whether the new keyboard was supposed to sound like a small machine shop. I said I was not sure. By the end of the week I had stopped hearing it, in the way you stop hearing your refrigerator. By the end of the second week she had stopped hearing it too, which I think is the more interesting phenomenon.

What I will say in conclusion is this. I have used a lot of keyboards in my life and I have never written a paragraph about any of them. That this one has produced fifteen hundred words of self-observation says less about the keyboard, probably, and more about what happens when you change a tool you use for eight hours a day. The tools shape the output and the tools shape your attention to the output. Most of the time you do not notice this, because the tool has not changed.

For three weeks, mine has changed.
